    • Case Laws

    Understanding these landmark cases is crucial for proper law enforcement. Each case establishes important precedents that guide our daily operations.

    Miranda v. Arizona

    Any statement made in custody is admissible in court if the Miranda rights have been stated and dismissed or exercised.

    Tennessee v. Garner

    A police officer may use deadly force to prevent the escape of a fleeing suspect only if the officer has a good-faith belief that the suspect poses a significant threat of death or serious physical injury to the officer or others.

    Terry v. Ohio

    An officer with reasonable suspicion and the good faith believe that the suspect is armed and dangerous, may conduct a 'stop and frisk' on a suspect without probable cause to arrest. The 'Terry Frisk' is a frisk of a person for any weapons that they have on their person.

    Pennsylvania v. Mimms

    An officer may order a driver out of an automobile after a lawfully detained traffic stop under the justification of officers safety. Officers are not required to give any other justification for a detained individual to step out of a vehicle other than for officers safety. So long as the reason for the stop is lawful, this request is a lawful order.

    Carroll v. United States

    The "automobile exception" to the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement allows warrantless searches of vehicles based on probable cause, due to their mobility. If an officer has made the determination that a crime has been, will be, or is being committed based on evidence, facts or circumstances, they can search a vehicle without the need of a search & seizure warrant solely for the piece of evidence related to the crime.

    Motorhomes can be searched without the need of a warrant if the officers have PC that there is evidence of a crime in said motorhome. (The exception to this are motorhomes with the lack of wheels that are supported by bricks and/or pillars.)

    Wyoming v. Houghton

    Ruled that police officers with probable cause to search a car may also search any container within the vehicle, including those belonging to passengers, that could conceal the object of the search.

    United States v. Ross

    The court ruled that police officers with probable cause to believe a vehicle contains contraband may conduct a warrantless search of the vehicle, including closed containers, as thorough as a magistrate could authorize with a warrant.

    California v. Carney

    A motorhome meets the criteria for the automobile exception and may be searched without a warrant if there is probable cause to believe evidence is present within.

    United States v. Ludwig

    A narcotics dog alert to a defendant's car provided probable cause for a warrantless search of the car's trunk, emphasizing the reliability of dog alerts in establishing probable cause.

    Scott v. Harris

    A police officer's attempt to terminate a dangerous high-speed car chase, even if it places the fleeing motorist at risk of serious injury or death, does not violate the Fourth Amendment if the officer's actions are objectively reasonable.

    Garrity v. New Jersey - "Garrity Warning"

    Public employees retain their Fifth Amendment right to remain silent and cannot be compelled to give statements that could incriminate them. Statements compelled under a threat of job loss are inadmissible in a criminal trial. Public employees can still be compelled to make statements for internal, administrative purposes, and these compelled statements can be used for disciplinary actions, including termination.

    Graham v. Connor

    Claims of excessive force by law enforcement during arrests, investigatory stops, or other seizures should be analyzed under the Fourth Amendment's "objective reasonableness" standard, not a due process standard.

    Mapp v. Ohio

    Evidence seized unlawfully, without a search warrant, could not be used in criminal prosecutions in state courts.

    Davis v. United States

    Searches conducted in objectively reasonable reliance on binding appellate precedent are not subject to the exclusionary rule.

    Maryland v. King

    The United States Supreme Court decided that a cheek swab of an arrestee's DNA is comparable to fingerprinting and therefore, a legal police booking procedure that is reasonable under the Fourth Amendment only when charging the arrestee with a violent crime. The individual must have committed a felony.

    These case laws form the foundation of modern law enforcement procedures. Understanding them is crucial for making sound decisions in the field.
    Failure to follow these established precedents can result in evidence being thrown out and cases being dismissed.

    Stop & Go Traffic Rule

    • Drivers must come to a complete stop at every red light.
    • After stopping, they may continue through the light only if the intersection is safe and clear of traffic.

    Illegal Parking

    A vehicle is illegally parked if it is:

    • In a no-parking zone (red curbs, posted signs)
    • Within 5 feet of a fire hydrant
    • Blocking a driveway or property entrance without the owner's consent (must be confirmed at the time)
    • Taking up more than one parking space
    • Unreasonably blocking traffic
    • Left in an area not designed for motor traffic
    • Facing the wrong way of travel
    • Left abandoned in the roadway, unless the driver is actively awaiting roadside assistance

    Parking Exemptions

    • State agency vehicles, while actively engaged in their duties
    • Tow trucks engaged in parking enforcement or recovery work
    • Any vehicle lacking a motor (pedal bicycles, skateboards, etc)

    Self-Defense Requirements

    Every person must meet ALL of the following requirements for the affirmative defense of Self-Defense to stand:

    • Not the initial aggressor - Person claiming self-defense cannot have engaged in assault or battery against the claimed assailant before being attacked
    • Unlawful attack - The threat faced must have come from an unlawful, unreasonable action
    • Necessity - The harm being faced must've been a clear and serious threat to one's safety or life
    • Reasonableness - The force used was a reasonable amount needed to stop the threat
    • Accountability - As soon as safely possible, the person must have reached law enforcement and reported the incident

    Burden of Proof

    The degree of doubt that must be overcome in a legal proceeding by the party bringing the case:

    • Criminal Court Case - Beyond Reasonable Doubt (over 90%)
    • Bench Trial - Clear and Convincing (over 75%)
    • Civil Court Case - Preponderance of Evidence (over 50%)

    Statute of Limitations

    Timeframe within which a criminal charge or a case has to be brought to the court, starting on the day of the initial incident:

    • Civil Cases - 21 days (for Civilian to file a civil suit)
    • Criminal Charge - 14 days* (for Law Enforcement to press charge)
    • Criminal / Ruling Appeal - 14 days (for People to appeal criminal charges or trial ruling)
    • Hold Until Trial Charge - No statute of limitations. However, once the suspect is in State Custody, charges must be finalized within 72 hours of the arrest
    • Court Rulings - No statute of limitations
    * - In Special Investigation Cases, the Chief Justice may grant extensions to the statute of limitations at their discretion.

    Double Jeopardy

    Double Jeopardy Clause prohibits anyone from being prosecuted twice for substantially the same crime. Only sanctions which can be considered as "Punishment" would qualify under the rule.

    Delayed Death Exception

    • Double Jeopardy doesn't apply in the case of of a delayed death where the person dies from injuries after prosecution.
    • In the event of a violent felony resulting in a conviction whereby the victim passes away as a result of the initial violent act, regardless of the amount of time that has passed, the Defendant will not be protected by the Double Jeopardy Clause.

    Training Success Tips

    Professional Conduct

  • Wear professional attire appropriate for law enforcement
  • Maintain a positive, engaged attitude
  • Be prepared to answer any question confidently
  • Stay adaptable and avoid rigid thinking
  • Bring your sense of humor - a good attitude helps learning
    • While professionalism is key, don't be overly rigid or restricted. The best officers can balance professionalism with approachability.

    Physical Preparation

  • Maintain good physical fitness
  • Practice weapon handling regularly
  • Get adequate rest before training
  • Stay hydrated during exercises

    • Mental Preparation

  • Study procedures before practical exercises
  • Ask questions when unclear
  • Learn from mistakes
  • Stay focused during scenarios
    • Take detailed notes during briefings and debriefings. Review them regularly.
